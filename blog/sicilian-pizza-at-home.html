Sicilian Pizza: Thick, Focaccia-Style, and Nothing Like What Most People Think

Most people who think they’ve had Sicilian pizza haven’t had real Sicilian pizza. They’ve had a thick rectangular pizza from a chain that labels things Sicilian because they’re square. That’s not Sicilian.

Real Sicilian pizza — sfincione from Palermo — is a thick, focaccia-like dough topped with a slow-cooked sauce of tomatoes, anchovies, onions, and breadcrumbs. No mozzarella, or very little. Strong, savory, complex. It’s been made in Sicilian households for centuries.

What most Americans call Sicilian is the Italian-American version — thick, rectangular, bready, with standard tomato sauce and mozzarella, baked in an oiled pan until the bottom is golden. That’s a legitimate and delicious pizza. It’s just not Sicilian in the traditional sense.

Both are worth knowing. This guide covers both.

Traditional Sfincione vs. American Sicilian

Sfincione (Palermo): The original. Thick focaccia-style dough, high hydration (70%+), long fermentation. Topped with a cooked sauce of canned tomatoes, sautéed onions, anchovies, and breadcrumbs. Caciocavallo cheese if any. Baked in an olive oil-coated pan until the bottom is deeply fried and the top is golden. The breadcrumbs on top are toasted and savory. No mozzarella.

American Sicilian: Thick rectangular dough, moderately high hydration (65-70%), standard tomato sauce, generous mozzarella, toppings of choice. The defining feature is the thick, airy crumb and fried bottom. This is what most Italian-American pizzerias make.

For home bakers, the American version is more accessible. The sfincione is worth making once you have the American version down.

What Defines American Sicilian at Home

The pan: Heavy aluminum or steel rectangular pan, well-oiled. The pan has deeper sides than a Grandma pan — usually 1-1.5 inches deep. This contains the thick dough.

The dough: Thick, open-crumbed, tender. The high hydration and longer fermentation create a dough that bakes up airy inside and fried outside. This is not a crispy pizza — it’s bready and substantial.

The bake: 450°F, lower rack, long enough for the bottom to develop color. The internal crumb needs to be fully baked through — this takes longer than thinner styles. 20-30 minutes is typical.

The bottom: Golden and slightly fried from the oil. This should crunch when you press it. If it’s pale and soft, it needed more oil or more time.

The Variables That Matter

Oil in the pan: Same principle as Grandma and Detroit — more than you think. The oil fries the bottom. Use olive oil for flavor.

Hydration (65-70%): High enough to create the open crumb. Weigh your ingredients.

Fermentation: This dough benefits enormously from a long cold ferment. Same-day Sicilian is edible. 48-hour cold-ferment Sicilian is genuinely good.

Dough weight per pan: More dough than you’d think. You want the baked crust to be 1-1.5 inches tall. Most home bakers under-portion and get a thin result.
