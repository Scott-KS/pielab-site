St. Louis Pizza: Provel, Party Cuts, and the Style That Refuses to Apologize

Cracker-thin crust. Processed cheese that’s not really cheese. Cut into squares. Outsiders ask if it’s even pizza. St. Louis doesn’t care.

Every year, somewhere on the internet, a national food publication discovers St. Louis pizza for the first time and writes a piece about how strange it is. Cracker-thin crust. A processed cheese product that doesn’t exist anywhere else. Cut into squares instead of triangles. Sauce that’s a little sweet. People in the comments fight about whether it qualifies as pizza at all.

St. Louis people read these articles, shrug, and order another Imo’s. They’ve been doing this for seventy years and they don’t need anyone’s permission.

This is the most defiantly local pizza style in America. It is not trying to be Neapolitan. It is not trying to be New York. It is not trying to be anything except itself, and it’s very good at that.

Where It Comes From

St. Louis’s pizza identity is essentially the story of one family and one cheese.

The family is the Imo family, who opened Imo’s Pizza in 1964 and built it into a regional chain that defines the style for most St. Louisans the way Pepe’s defines New Haven and Buddy’s defines Detroit. Ed and Margie Imo did not invent the style — thin-crust pizza had been served on the Hill, St. Louis’s historic Italian neighborhood, for years before they opened. But Imo’s standardized it, scaled it, and shipped it to suburbs across the metro area, and the style became inseparable from the brand.

The cheese is Provel, and Provel is the part that makes everyone else lose their minds.

Provel is a processed cheese product made by combining cheddar, Swiss, and provolone, then processing them together into a smooth, low-melting-point cheese with a distinctive smoky flavor. It’s registered as “Provel” and it’s manufactured almost exclusively for the St. Louis market by a small number of producers, the most famous being Costa Grocery and Roma Cheese. Outside St. Louis, you basically can’t buy it. Inside St. Louis, it’s the only cheese on the pizza.

What Defines the Style

The crust. Thin to the point of being a cracker. Made without yeast in the traditional version — the dough is essentially flour, water, salt, and oil, mixed and rolled out the same day, no fermentation. It’s less a bread and more a flat carrier. When it bakes, it crisps all the way through. There’s no chew. You don’t fold a slice. You don’t need to.

Provel cheese. Pale yellow, smooth, low melt. When it cooks it doesn’t stretch — it gets gooey and slightly sticky, almost like queso. The flavor is mild, sweet, smoky. It tastes nothing like mozzarella. People who love it love it specifically because it doesn’t taste like normal pizza cheese. People who hate it usually hate it on the first bite without giving it a second.

Sweet sauce. St. Louis pizza sauce leans noticeably sweeter than New York or Neapolitan sauce. Often made with a touch of sugar or with a sweeter tomato base. The sweetness pairs with the saltiness of the Provel and the crispness of the crust to create the flavor profile St. Louisans grew up calibrating their tastebuds to.

The party cut. Squares. Always squares. The pizza comes out of the oven and gets cut into small rectangles or squares with a wheel cutter, never triangles. This is called a “party cut” or a “tavern cut.” The advantage is that more people can grab a piece without making a commitment to a full slice, and the small portions match the cracker crust — a triangle slice of cracker-thin pizza would just be a long, thin piece of cardboard.

Toppings to the edge. Unlike most American pizza, which leaves a bare crust border, St. Louis pizza has cheese and toppings extending all the way to the rim. Every bite is identical. There is no “crust piece” left over.

The Provel Question

You cannot write about St. Louis pizza without addressing Provel, because Provel is the entire conversation outside St. Louis.

Critics call it processed cheese product, dismiss it as inauthentic, and argue that it disqualifies St. Louis pizza from being “real” pizza. They’re technically correct that Provel is processed and that it bears no resemblance to traditional Italian cheeses. They’re also missing the point.

Provel is to St. Louis pizza what Velveeta is to queso. The fact that it’s engineered for a specific function doesn’t make it worse — it makes it the right tool for the job the rest of the pizza is doing.

The cracker crust needs a low-melting cheese. A cheese that stretches like mozzarella would tear the crust apart when you tried to cut it. A cheese that browns like provolone would dry out before the crust crisped. Provel was engineered — or more accurately, evolved over decades of pizzerias adjusting their cheese blend — to do exactly what the rest of the pizza needs. It melts evenly, stays put when cut, and contributes a smoky-sweet flavor that complements rather than competes.

If you visit St. Louis and try a real Imo’s pie next to a homemade attempt with regular mozzarella, the difference is immediate. The mozzarella version isn’t St. Louis pizza. It’s thin-crust pizza with mozzarella on it. The Provel makes it the thing.

Making It At Home

The challenge for home bakers outside the St. Louis metro is exactly one ingredient: Provel.

You have three options:

  1. Order it. Real Provel is sold by Costa Grocery and other St. Louis-area shops that ship nationally. This is the only way to make a truly authentic pie. Expect to pay shipping that exceeds the cost of the cheese.
  2. Mix your own. The traditional substitute is a 1:1:1 blend of white cheddar, Swiss, and provolone, finely shredded and mixed thoroughly. It doesn’t replicate Provel exactly — the smoothness and the smoky note will be slightly off — but it gets the flavor profile within range. A small splash of liquid smoke in the sauce gets you closer.
  3. Skip the pretense. Use whatever cheese you have, accept that it won’t taste like Imo’s, and enjoy a thin-crust square-cut pizza on its own terms. This is what most people end up doing.

The crust is the easy part. A no-yeast dough is one of the simplest things you can make:

  • 2 cups (240g) all-purpose flour
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 cup (120g) cold water
  • 2 tablespoons olive oil

Mix until it forms a stiff dough, knead for two minutes, let it rest covered for 15 minutes, then roll it out as thin as you can on a lightly floured surface. You want it almost translucent. Transfer to a pizza screen or a parchment-lined steel.

Top with a sweet tomato sauce (crushed tomatoes, salt, a pinch of sugar, oregano, garlic powder), your cheese to the edge, and any toppings. Bake at maximum oven temperature (550°F if you can) on a preheated steel until the crust is deeply browned and the cheese is bubbling. Six to eight minutes for most home ovens.

Cut with a pizza wheel into squares. Eat immediately while it’s still crisp.

The Variables That Matter

Crust thickness. Thinner is better. If you can see daylight through the dough as you roll it, you’re close. If it tears slightly, that’s OK — pinch it back together and keep going. A thicker crust changes the entire eating experience.

Cheese to the edge. No bare border. Every bite is the same bite. This is part of the design.

Sauce sweetness. Don’t skip the pinch of sugar in the sauce. St. Louis sauce is meaningfully sweeter than other styles. It’s not heavy-handed, but it’s noticeable.

Bake hot, bake fast. The crust needs to crisp completely before the cheese overcooks. Maximum oven temp, preheated steel, six to eight minutes. If your bake takes twelve minutes, your oven isn’t hot enough.

Cut while hot. A pizza wheel works best on a fresh, hot pizza. Wait too long and the cheese sets up enough to drag and tear when you cut.

Tracking Your St. Louis Bakes

St. Louis-style is one of the few pizzas where the dough is essentially a constant — no fermentation variables, no hydration adjustments, no bulk-rise timing. The variables you’re tracking are different: cheese blend ratios, sauce sweetness, rolled crust thickness, bake intensity. These are subtle but they compound over time, and the journal is where you find your version of the style.

If you’re iterating on a Provel substitute, write down the ratios and your impression. The version with extra Swiss tastes different from the version with extra cheddar. The version with a smoke ingredient tastes different from the version without. After five or six bakes, you’ll have your house blend.

The Reason This Style Matters

Every regional pizza style is a snapshot of a city’s priorities at the moment the style got fixed. New York wanted something you could fold and eat walking. Detroit wanted something you could bake in a steel pan from the auto plant. New Haven wanted to do justice to a coal oven. Chicago tavern style wanted small portions you could share with friends at a bar.

St. Louis wanted a pizza that worked for a party. Light enough that you could eat ten squares without filling up. Cut into pieces small enough that everyone could grab one. Topped with a cheese designed not to drip or string. Sauce sweet enough that kids would eat it. Crust crisp enough that it didn’t go soggy on a serving tray.

Every choice that outsiders criticize about St. Louis pizza is actually a deliberate engineering decision in service of the same goal: make the pizza that people in St. Louis actually want to eat at the parties they actually have. And after seventy years, they’re still ordering it, the kids who grew up on it are ordering it for their kids, and Imo’s is still open.

That’s a more impressive achievement than any pizza style that’s trying to impress New York food critics.
