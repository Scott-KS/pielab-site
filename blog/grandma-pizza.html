Every pizza style has a champion. Neapolitan has the purists. Detroit has the converts. New York has everyone. Grandma pizza has the people who’ve actually had it — and they don’t understand why the rest of the world isn’t paying more attention.

Grandma pizza is a Long Island thing. It’s made in a rectangular pan, but it’s thinner and crispier than Sicilian. The bottom is fried in olive oil against the steel pan, similar to Detroit but lighter. The sauce goes on top of the cheese — or in some versions, gets added raw directly to the dough before the cheese, creating a \"red pie\" look. The crust has structure but not the bready thickness of deep pan styles.

It’s been made in home kitchens on Long Island for decades, which is literally where the name comes from. Grandma made it in a sheet pan because it was easy, it fed a crowd, and it didn’t require any special equipment.

What Defines Grandma Pizza

The pan: Standard half-sheet pan (13x18 inches) or a 9x13 baking pan, well-oiled with olive oil. The olive oil matters — it’s part of the flavor and the fry.

The crust: Thinner than Sicilian, thicker than New York. The interior should be tender and open. The bottom should be golden and slightly fried. The top gets light browning from the oven.

The cheese: Low-moisture mozzarella, applied generously. Some versions use shredded, some use sliced. The cheese should cover the surface fully.

The sauce: Often goes on top of the cheese in the Grandma style, creating a distinctive appearance where you see the red sauce sitting on top of the browned cheese. This is different from most pizza styles and is part of what makes it visually distinctive.

The oil: More olive oil than you think. The dough gets dimpled and olive-oiled before baking, similar to focaccia. This is what creates the fried bottom.

Why Grandma Pizza Is Perfect for Home Bakers

No shaping skill required. The dough goes in the pan and spreads on its own — you press and dimple it like focaccia. No peel, no stretching over knuckles, no worrying about holes. The pan contains everything.

The bake is straightforward. 450-475°F on a lower rack for 20-25 minutes. You can see what’s happening and adjust. No launch timing, no worrying about whether the peel will stick.

The results are impressive relative to the effort. Grandma pizza looks great, tastes deeply savory from the olive oil fry, and feeds a crowd from a single pan. It’s the highest effort-to-reward ratio of almost any pizza style.

The Variables That Matter

Olive oil quantity: Be generous. You want at least 3-4 tablespoons coating the pan, with more drizzled over the dough when you dimple it. Less oil produces a soft, bread-like bottom instead of a fried one.

Dough hydration (65-70%): Higher hydration than New York, similar to Detroit. The slack dough is what creates the open, tender crumb after baking.

Cold fermentation: 24-48 hours cold gives you a better flavor and better crumb structure. Same-day works but tastes flatter.

Dimpling: Press your fingers into the dough all over the surface after it’s settled in the pan. This creates texture on top and helps the olive oil pool. It’s also how you know it’s relaxed enough to bake.

Sauce placement: Traditional Grandma has sauce on top of the cheese. Try it this way first before experimenting with under-cheese placement.
