Deep Dish Is Tourist Pizza. Chicago's Real Pizza Is Thin.

This is going to make some people angry. Good.

This is going to make some people angry. Good.

Chicago has a pizza identity problem that it created for itself, and it's time to correct the record. The pizza that gets exported to the rest of the world as "Chicago pizza" — the casserole in a pan, the pizza you eat with a fork, the pizza that takes 45 minutes to bake because it's essentially a meat pie — is not what Chicagoans eat.

Tourists eat deep dish. Chicagoans eat thin.

The Data Is Not Ambiguous

Pizzeria Uno opened in 1943. Pizzeria Due opened in 1955. Giordano's opened in 1974. Lou Malnati's, the most famous name in Chicago deep dish, opened in 1971.

Tavern-style pizza was being made in Chicago bars decades before any of those. The style traces to the mid-20th century bar culture of Chicago's neighborhoods — the places where people actually ate after work, after games, after everything.

Today, tavern-style pizzerias significantly outnumber deep dish pizzerias in Chicago. When Chicagoans have pizza on a Tuesday, they're not ordering deep dish. They're calling Pequod's or Pat's or Vito & Nick's or any of the hundred neighborhood spots that serve thin, square-cut, crispy-edged pizza the way it's been served for generations.

Deep dish is what you show out-of-town visitors because it's dramatic and photogenic. It's a tourist experience. Locals have it occasionally, the way New Yorkers occasionally eat a supersized slice from a tourist trap on Times Square.

What Tavern-Style Actually Is

Cracker-thin crust. Rolled out, not tossed. Docked so it stays flat. Topped edge to edge — sausage pressed flat all the way to the rim, no bare crust showing anywhere. Square cut into party-sized pieces. Served at a bar with a beer.

The flavor profile is savory and straightforward. Fennel-heavy sausage, provolone-mozzarella blend, simple tomato sauce. The crust is there to carry the toppings, not compete with them.

It's not a complicated pizza. It's not a showpiece pizza. It's a pizza you can eat six pieces of while watching a game and then order another.

That's what Chicago pizza actually is.

Why This Matters for Home Bakers

Tavern-style is underrepresented in home pizza content because it doesn't have the visual drama of Neapolitan leopard char or the engineering spectacle of deep dish. It looks simple. It is simple — in execution. In results, it's deeply satisfying and harder to get right than it looks.

The cracker crust is the challenge. Hydration needs to be low (50-55%). The dough needs to be rolled thin and even. It needs to be docked properly. The bake temperature and surface need to be right. Getting all of these variables aligned produces a crust that's rigid, savory, and worth eating on its own.

Home bakers who master tavern-style have something most pizza enthusiasts don't: a style they can make well that's genuinely under-the-radar and produces a result that's better than what most people have ever had.
